Stop Being Misled by UV Rejection: The Only Number That Tells You How Hot Your Room Will Get

By ClearGuard Solutions  |  LLumar Approved Installer, Helderberg  |  March 2026

Walk into any window tinting company in South Africa and ask about heat rejection. Within seconds, you'll hear it: "Our film blocks 99% of UV rays." It sounds impressive. It's printed on every brochure, quoted in every sales pitch, and repeated in every Google ad. And it is, technically, true.

But here's what they're not telling you: UV radiation accounts for only about 3% of the sun's total heat energy. Blocking 99% of 3% means you've addressed less than 3% of the problem. Your room will still bake. Your air conditioner will still run all day. Your furniture will still fade. And you'll still be wondering why you spent the money.

"UV rejection tells you how well a film protects your skin and furnishings from ultraviolet light. It tells you almost nothing about how much heat the film actually blocks."

Where Does Solar Heat Actually Come From?

The sun's energy reaches us across three distinct wavelength bands, each contributing differently to the heat you feel in your room:

Solar Energy Composition
UV
Visible Light (44%)
Infrared / Heat (53%)
UV: 3% Visible: 44% Infrared: 53%

The infrared (IR) band — the invisible heat radiation — is responsible for over half of the solar heat entering your building. Visible light contributes another 44%. UV? Just 3%. So when a film proudly blocks 99% of UV, it's addressing the smallest slice of the pie.

What Is TSER and Why Does It Change Everything?

Total Solar Energy Rejected (TSER) is the single figure that accounts for all three bands — UV, visible light, and infrared — combined. It tells you what percentage of the sun's total energy a film prevents from entering your space.

The Simple Rule

A film with a TSER of 79% blocks 79% of all solar heat — UV, visible, and infrared combined. A film with a TSER of 21% blocks only 21%, regardless of what its UV rejection figure says. Always ask for TSER.

Consider two films side by side. Both claim 99% UV rejection. Film A has a TSER of 21%. Film B has a TSER of 79%. Film A will leave your room nearly as hot as an untinted window. Film B will make a dramatic, noticeable difference to your comfort and your electricity bill. The UV figure told you nothing useful. The TSER figure told you everything.

The IR Rejection Trap

Some companies, aware that UV rejection is losing credibility, have shifted to quoting IR rejection instead. This is better than UV — but it's still not the full picture. IR rejection only measures performance against the infrared band, ignoring visible light, which carries 44% of solar heat. A film can have impressive IR rejection but mediocre TSER if it doesn't also manage visible light transmission effectively.

TSER is the only figure that gives you the complete picture across all three energy bands.

What TSER Should You Look For?

As a general guide for South African conditions:

Below 40% TSER — Minimal heat reduction. Suitable only for mild climates or where privacy is the primary goal.
40–60% TSER — Moderate heat reduction. You'll notice a difference, but your air conditioner will still work hard.
60–75% TSER — Good heat reduction. Comfortable improvement in most rooms.
75%+ TSER — Excellent performance. This is where you see real energy savings and genuine comfort improvement. LLumar's EnerLogic range achieves up to 82% TSER.

The Bottom Line

Before you agree to any window film installation, ask one question: "What is the TSER of this film?" If the installer can't answer immediately, or deflects to UV rejection, that tells you everything you need to know about their expertise.
